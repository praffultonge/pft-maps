Chapter I

Preliminary

Regulation 1
Short title and commencement

(1) These regulations may be called the International Financial Services Centres Authority (Bullion Market) Regulations, 2025.

(2) These regulations shall come into force on the date of their notification in the Official Gazette.

Regulation 2
Definitions

(1) In these regulations, the following terms bear the meanings assigned below:

(a) "Act" — IFSCA Act, 2019 (50 of 2019);
(e) "bullion" — precious metals including gold, silver or other precious metals in the form of bars or unallocated gold, silver or other precious metals, as the Authority may consider relevant, relating to good delivery, quality, quantity, and any other aspect in relation to bullion trading;
(f) "bullion clearing corporation" — an entity which has been granted recognition under these regulations to undertake the activity of clearing and settlement of trades on a bullion exchange;
(g) "bullion clearing member" — an entity having clearing rights in any bullion clearing corporation and as such registered by the Authority;
(h) "bullion contract" — a contract for the purchase and sale of bullion, and includes bullion depository receipts, bullion products similar to BDRs, derivative contracts related to bullion, bullion spot delivery contracts, and any other bullion related contracts, as may be permitted by the Authority;
(i) "bullion depository" — an entity undertaking depository business in relation to securities including bullion depository receipts in an IFSC and regulated by the Authority;
(j) "bullion depository receipt (BDR)" — a receipt created by a bullion depository and listed on a bullion exchange;
(k) "bullion exchange" — an entity which has been granted recognition by the Authority for the purpose of assisting, regulating or controlling the business of buying, selling and dealing of BDRs and derivative contracts on bullion in an IFSC;
(l) "bullion market" — includes the bullion exchanges, bullion clearing corporations, bullion depositories, vaults, bullion contracts dealt therein, and any other entities connected thereto;
(m) "bullion trading member" — an entity having trading rights in a bullion exchange;
(n) "consumer" or "client" — a constituent of a bullion trading member and includes special category client or such other persons as may be specified by the Authority;
(v) "qualified jeweller" — a person notified as a Qualified Jeweller by the Ministry of Commerce and Industry, Govt. of India;
(w) "qualified supplier" — a person registered as a Qualified Supplier by the Authority;
(aa) "vault" — any approved premises where the vault manager deposits the bullion accepted from depositers and includes other places approved by the Authority for storage of bullion;
(bb) "vault manager" — an NCFE registered with the Authority and listed with a bullion depository, having clearing rights to manage the vault business;
Explanation For the purposes of sub-clause (bb), "NCFE" (Non-Corporate Financial Entity) includes banks, NBFCs and other entities as the Authority may specify.
Chapter II

Recognition of Bullion Exchange and Bullion Clearing Corporation

Regulation 3
Application for Recognition

(1) Any applicant desirous of obtaining recognition as a bullion exchange or bullion clearing corporation shall make an application to the Authority in the prescribed form and manner, along with applicable fees.

(2) Each application shall include: (a) details of ownership structure; (b) proposed governance framework; (c) details of fit and proper persons; (d) proposed technological infrastructure; (e) financial projections and net worth compliance; (f) any other information the Authority may require.

(3) The Authority may call for additional information or clarifications before processing the application.

Regulation 4
Conditions for Grant of Recognition

(1) An applicant seeking recognition as a bullion exchange or bullion clearing corporation shall comply with the following conditions:

(a) the applicant is a company limited by shares;
(b) the applicant is demutualised — i.e., ownership and management of the applicant is segregated from the trading rights or clearing rights, as applicable;
(c) the applicant, its directors and shareholders who hold or intend to hold shares, are fit and proper persons;
(d) the applicant satisfies the conditions relating to ownership and governance structure as specified in these regulations;
(e) the applicant satisfies the net worth requirements as specified in these regulations;
(f) the applicant has the requisite capability including financial capacity, functional expertise and infrastructure.

(2) An applicant seeking recognition as a bullion exchange shall additionally comply with:

(a) necessary infrastructure for the orderly execution of trades;
(b) an online screen-based trading system;
(c) online surveillance capability monitoring positions, prices and volumes in real time to ensure market integrity;
(d) adequate infrastructure to list BDRs for trading, wherever applicable;
(e) capability to have a comprehensive network of bullion trading members;
(f) arrangements to establish connectivity with trading members and BCC;
(g) adequate consumer grievance redressal mechanism and arbitration mechanism;
(h) facility to disseminate information about trades, quantities and quotes in real time to at least two information vending networks accessible to consumers;
(i) adequate systems' capacity with a business continuity plan including a disaster recovery site.

(3) An applicant seeking recognition as a bullion clearing corporation shall additionally comply with:

(a) necessary infrastructure to ensure timely clearing and settlement of trades;
(b) an adequate risk management mechanism;
(c) a settlement guarantee fund;
(d) back-up facilities and systems for settlement.
Regulation 5
Grant of Recognition

(1) The Authority shall, after considering the application and satisfying itself that the applicant complies with the requirements of these regulations, grant recognition to the applicant as a bullion exchange or bullion clearing corporation.

(2) The Authority may impose such conditions while granting recognition as it deems fit, in the interests of the bullion market and investors.

(3) The Authority may reject an application where the applicant does not fulfil the requirements, with reasons recorded in writing.

(4) Recognition granted under this regulation shall be valid for such period as the Authority may specify and shall be subject to renewal.

Regulation 6
Net Worth Requirement — Bullion Exchange

(1) A bullion exchange shall at all times maintain a minimum net worth as may be specified by the Authority from time to time.

(2) The Authority has, by circular, prescribed the minimum net worth at USD 5 million for a bullion exchange.

(3) The bullion exchange shall submit audited financial statements and net worth certificates to the Authority at such periodicity as specified.

Regulation 7
Net Worth Requirement — Bullion Clearing Corporation

(1) A bullion clearing corporation shall at all times maintain a minimum net worth as may be specified by the Authority.

(2) The Authority has prescribed the minimum net worth at USD 10 million for a bullion clearing corporation.

(3) The BCC shall also maintain a Settlement Guarantee Fund at such levels as specified by the Authority from time to time.

Regulation 8
Governance Requirements

(1) The governing board of a bullion exchange or bullion clearing corporation shall consist of:

(a) shareholder directors (not exceeding 50% of total directors);
(b) trading member / clearing member directors (not exceeding 25%);
(c) public interest directors as specified by the Authority.

(2) The majority of the governing board shall consist of public interest directors.

(3) The Managing Director / CEO and other KMPs shall be appointed in accordance with the fit and proper criteria specified by the Authority.

Regulation 9
Public Interest Directors

(1) The Authority shall nominate public interest directors on the governing board of every bullion exchange and bullion clearing corporation.

(2) Public interest directors shall represent the interests of investors and the securities market generally and shall have no direct or indirect connection with the exchange or clearing corporation that would conflict with their role.

(3) A public interest director shall hold office for such period as the Authority may determine and shall be eligible for re-nomination.

Regulation 10
Listing of Bullion Contracts

(1) A bullion exchange may permit the listing and trading of bullion contracts, including BDRs, bullion spot delivery contracts, derivative contracts related to bullion, and any other contracts as may be permitted by the Authority.

(2) The bullion exchange shall frame regulations governing the listing of bullion contracts, subject to the approval of the Authority.

(3) The specifications of each bullion contract, including the underlying asset, lot size, delivery mechanism, tick size, margin requirements and settlement procedure, shall be specified in the contract specifications approved by the Authority.

(4) Bullion contracts may be cash-settled or physically settled, as specified in the contract specifications.

Regulation 11
Suspension and Delisting of Contracts

(1) A bullion exchange may, subject to prior approval of the Authority, suspend or delist a bullion contract where continued trading would be detrimental to the interests of market participants or would otherwise be contrary to the interests of the bullion market.

(2) Where the Authority is satisfied that continued trading of a bullion contract poses systemic risk, it may direct the exchange to suspend or delist such contract.

Regulation 12
Ownership and Shareholding Restrictions

(1) No person shall, either individually or together with associates, acquire or hold more than 15% of the paid-up equity share capital of a bullion exchange or bullion clearing corporation without prior approval of the Authority.

(2) No trading member or clearing member or associate of such member shall hold more than 5% of the paid-up equity share capital of the bullion exchange or the bullion clearing corporation in which they have trading or clearing rights.

(3) Any acquisition of shares beyond the limits specified shall be subject to approval and such conditions as the Authority may impose.

Explanation — Demutualisation Logic Sub-regulation (2) operationalises the demutualisation requirement in Reg 4(b). It prevents a trading member from simultaneously being a significant owner of the exchange — eliminating the structural conflict between commercial interest and regulatory function.
Regulation 13
Regulatory Compliance

(1) A bullion exchange and bullion clearing corporation shall, at all times, comply with the provisions of the Act, Securities Contracts (Regulation) Act, 1956, Depositories Act, 1956, these regulations and all circulars, guidelines, directions, and orders issued by the Authority.

(2) The bullion exchange shall have in place a comprehensive risk management framework covering market risk, operational risk, credit risk, and systemic risk, as approved by the Authority.

(3) The bullion exchange and the BCC shall have a mechanism to detect and prevent manipulative and fraudulent practices, including price manipulation, wash trades, and front-running.

Regulation 14
Renewal of Recognition

(1) An application for renewal of recognition shall be made in the prescribed form and manner, along with the requisite fees, at least three months before the expiry of the existing recognition.

(2) The Authority shall renew the recognition if it is satisfied that the entity continues to comply with the conditions of initial recognition and any additional conditions that may have been imposed.

(3) Where an entity fails to apply for renewal within the prescribed period, the recognition shall be deemed to have lapsed and the entity shall not carry on the business of a bullion exchange or BCC.

Chapter III

Bullion Trading Members and Clearing Members

Regulation 15
Eligibility — Bullion Trading Members

(1) The following persons are eligible to become bullion trading members of a bullion exchange, subject to compliance with the conditions specified in these regulations:

(a) an IBU (IFSC Banking Unit) regulated under the IFSCA (Banking) Regulations;
(b) a Finance Company registered under IFSCA (Finance Company) Regulations;
(c) a broker-dealer regulated in a foreign jurisdiction;
(d) a bullion refiner or producer;
(e) any other entity as may be specified by the Authority from time to time.

(2) Every bullion trading member shall obtain registration from the Authority before commencing trading on the bullion exchange.

Regulation 16
Registration of Bullion Trading Members

(1) An applicant seeking registration as a bullion trading member shall make an application to the Authority through the bullion exchange, in such form and manner and along with such fees as may be specified.

(2) The applicant shall, inter alia, satisfy the following conditions:

(a) is a fit and proper person;
(b) satisfies the net worth requirement as specified;
(c) has adequate infrastructure and operational capacity;
(d) has adequate internal systems, controls and risk management processes.

(3) The bullion exchange shall make a recommendation to the Authority regarding the suitability of the applicant within 30 days of receipt of the application.

Regulation 17
Net Worth and Margins — Trading Members

(1) Every bullion trading member shall at all times maintain the minimum net worth as may be specified by the Authority from time to time.

(2) The Authority has, by circulars, prescribed the following minimum net worth:

(a) Trading-cum-clearing member (TCM): USD 1.5 million;
(b) Professional clearing member (PCM): USD 3 million;
(c) Self-clearing member: USD 1 million.

(3) The bullion exchange shall collect adequate margins from its trading members before commencement of trading, as prescribed by the Authority.

(4) A bullion trading member shall not take positions beyond the position limits specified by the bullion exchange, as approved by the Authority.

Regulation 18
Bullion Clearing Members

(1) The following entities may obtain registration as bullion clearing members:

(a) a bullion trading member who also wishes to undertake clearing activities (Trading-cum-Clearing Member);
(b) an entity that only undertakes clearing activities (Professional Clearing Member) without itself being a trading member;
(c) a trading member clearing only its own trades (Self-Clearing Member).

(2) Every bullion clearing member shall comply with the risk management requirements and margin obligations specified by the BCC, as approved by the Authority.

(3) The BCC shall have in place a comprehensive default management procedure for bullion clearing members who fail to meet their obligations.

Regulation 19
Rights and Duties of Trading Members

(1) A bullion trading member shall comply with all applicable circulars, guidelines and directions issued by the Authority and the bullion exchange.

(2) A bullion trading member shall:

(a) not enter into any bullion contract on behalf of a client unless the client has submitted KYC documentation as required;
(b) maintain separate client accounts and not use client funds for its own purposes;
(c) provide contract notes to clients within the specified time;
(d) report suspicious transactions in accordance with the AML/CFT framework.
Regulation 20
Authorised Persons

(1) A bullion trading member may appoint authorised persons to assist in carrying out its activities, subject to prior approval of the bullion exchange.

(2) An authorised person shall act within the scope authorised by the trading member and shall not independently execute trades.

(3) The bullion trading member shall be responsible for all acts and omissions of its authorised persons.

(4) An authorised person shall be required to comply with such conditions as may be specified by the Authority, including obtaining prior registration with the bullion exchange.

Regulation 21
Direct Market Access (DMA) and Sponsored Access

(1) A bullion exchange may, subject to the Authority's approval, provide Direct Market Access (DMA) facilities to eligible clients of trading members, enabling them to place orders directly into the exchange's trading system.

(2) A bullion exchange may provide Sponsored Access (SA) to entities that are not registered as trading members but are sponsored by a trading member, enabling direct access to the trading system through the trading member's infrastructure.

(3) The bullion exchange shall frame a comprehensive DMA/SA framework specifying eligibility criteria, risk controls, liability, position limits, and oversight mechanisms, subject to approval by the Authority.

Chapter IV

Bullion Depositories and Vault Managers

Regulation 22
Eligibility for Registration as Bullion Depository

(1) An applicant seeking registration as a bullion depository shall:

(a) be a company incorporated under the Companies Act, 2013;
(b) have, in its memorandum, the objects relating to carrying on the business of a depository;
(c) be promoted by a recognised depository in India, or by a depository registered in a foreign jurisdiction with a IOSCO-compliant regulator, or by such other entities as the Authority may specify;
(d) satisfy the net worth requirements as specified by the Authority;
(e) have the requisite infrastructure for maintaining accounts of beneficial owners and processing BDRs electronically.
Regulation 23
Registration of Bullion Depository

(1) An applicant meeting the eligibility criteria shall make an application to the Authority for registration as a bullion depository in the prescribed form.

(2) The Authority shall, after examining the application, register the entity as a bullion depository if satisfied that the conditions are met and the registration would be in the interests of the bullion market.

(3) The registered bullion depository shall maintain electronic records of BDRs held by beneficial owners and provide a platform for the creation, transfer, and extinguishment of BDRs.

(4) The functions of the bullion depository shall include: (a) issuance and maintenance of BDRs; (b) maintaining records of beneficial ownership; (c) interfacing with vault managers; (d) providing settlement infrastructure to the BCC.

Regulation 24
Registration of Vault Managers

(1) An entity seeking registration as a vault manager shall:

(a) be a company or an LLP incorporated in the IFSC, or a branch of a foreign company authorised to operate in the IFSC;
(b) have the vault premises located in the IFSC or in such other location as approved by the Authority;
(c) maintain adequate vaulting infrastructure meeting the good delivery standards;
(d) satisfy the net worth requirements and insurance requirements as specified by the Authority;
(e) be empanelled with at least one bullion depository.

(2) The vault manager shall execute a tripartite agreement among the depositor, the vault manager, and the bullion depository before accepting bullion for storage.

Regulation 25
Bullion Depository Receipt (BDR) — Issuance

(1) A BDR shall be created upon the deposit of physical bullion by a depositor with an approved vault manager, which shall be recorded by the bullion depository against the depositor's account.

(2) Each BDR shall represent a specific quantity of bullion meeting the good delivery standards and shall carry:

(a) unique identifier issued by the bullion depository;
(b) details of the vault and the vault manager;
(c) quantity, fineness and description of the underlying bullion;
(d) name of the beneficial owner.

(3) A BDR shall be listed on the bullion exchange and may be traded thereon in electronic form.

(4) The holder of a BDR may surrender the BDR and take delivery of the underlying physical bullion from the vault, subject to the delivery procedures specified by the bullion exchange and the bullion depository.

Tax Treatment Note BDRs were notified under section 47(viiaab) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 vide IFSCA Circular dated August 3, 2022 — transfers on a recognised bullion exchange attract no capital gains tax if subject to STT. This is the fiscal architecture enabling institutional participation.
Regulation 26
Vault Standards and Operations

(1) Every vault manager shall maintain the vault premises to the standards specified by the Authority, including:

(a) robust physical security systems;
(b) 24x7 CCTV surveillance;
(c) independent verification and auditing processes;
(d) adequate insurance covering the full replacement value of bullion stored.

(2) The vault manager shall conduct periodic audits and reconcile physical bullion stock against BDR records maintained by the bullion depository.

(3) The vault manager shall immediately report any discrepancy between physical stock and electronic records to the bullion depository and the Authority.

Regulation 27
Good Delivery Standards

(1) Only bullion that meets the good delivery standards specified by the Authority shall be accepted for deposit by a vault manager and represented in a BDR.

(2) The Authority has adopted, with modifications, the LBMA Good Delivery Standards for gold and silver, as the benchmark for bullion accepted on IIBX.

(3) Bullion from Qualified Suppliers (international refiners accredited on IIBX's qualified supplier list) meeting the standards shall be accepted without further assay. Bullion from other sources shall be subject to assay and re-branding at the depositor's cost.

Chapter V

Qualified Jewellers and Qualified Suppliers

Regulation 28
Qualified Jewellers — Access to IIBX

(1) A "Qualified Jeweller" means a person notified as such by the Ministry of Commerce and Industry, Government of India, or such other Ministry as designated by the Central Government.

(2) Qualified Jewellers may access the bullion exchange for the purpose of importing gold and silver through IIBX, subject to compliance with the conditions specified herein and in the applicable DGFT notifications, RBI regulations, and IFSCA circulars.

(3) The IFSCA shall specify, from time to time, the mechanism and conditions under which Qualified Jewellers may participate in the bullion exchange, including the size of lots for import, the payment mechanism, and the delivery process.

(4) The cumulative import limits for Qualified Jewellers through IIBX shall be as specified by the Ministry of Commerce and IFSCA jointly.

Regulation 29
Import of Gold and Silver by Qualified Jewellers

(1) A Qualified Jeweller desiring to import gold or silver through IIBX shall onboard itself with the bullion exchange as a client of a registered bullion trading member.

(2) The bullion exchange shall specify the eligible products, delivery schedules, lot sizes, and other terms for import by Qualified Jewellers.

(3) The proceeds of any transaction by a Qualified Jeweller through IIBX shall be routed through the banking channel and in compliance with the Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999 and RBI directions.

(4) Upon delivery of bullion to the Qualified Jeweller, the vault manager shall arrange for clearance through the designated customs authorities and delivery to the Qualified Jeweller's designated premises in India.

Regulation 30
Qualified Suppliers

(1) A "Qualified Supplier" means a person registered as a Qualified Supplier by the Authority and empanelled to supply bullion on IIBX.

(2) Any of the following entities may apply for registration as a Qualified Supplier:

(a) LBMA Good Delivery refiners;
(b) other internationally recognised refiners as may be specified by the Authority;
(c) bullion banks and bullion dealers in foreign jurisdictions regulated by an IOSCO-compliant regulator.

(3) A Qualified Supplier shall supply bullion meeting the good delivery standards and shall comply with the conditions specified by the Authority from time to time.

(4) Qualified Suppliers shall be permitted to hold unallocated bullion positions and to conduct transactions on IIBX as specified by the Authority.

Regulation 31
Obligations of Qualified Jewellers and Qualified Suppliers

(1) Every Qualified Jeweller and Qualified Supplier shall:

(a) comply with all applicable provisions of these regulations and directives issued by the Authority;
(b) maintain proper books of accounts in relation to their activities on IIBX;
(c) report such information to the Authority as may be specified from time to time;
(d) not engage in any market manipulation or fraudulent practices.

(2) The Authority may, by circular, specify additional obligations including AML/CFT compliance requirements applicable to Qualified Jewellers and Qualified Suppliers.

Chapter VI

Miscellaneous

Regulation 32
Inspection

(1) The Authority may appoint an inspecting authority to undertake inspection of the books of account, records and documents of any person regulated under these regulations.

(2) Such inspecting authority shall have access to all books of account, registers, documents, data and systems maintained by the regulated entity.

(3) The regulated entity shall extend full cooperation and provide all documents requested by the inspecting authority within the time specified.

Regulation 33
Penalties

(1) Without prejudice to the other provisions of these regulations and the Act, if any entity fails to comply with the provisions of these regulations or the directions of the Authority, the Authority may take such action or impose such penalty as may be prescribed under the Act.

(2) Penalties may include: (a) monetary penalty; (b) suspension or cancellation of recognition or registration; (c) prohibition from participating in the bullion market; (d) any other action as the Authority deems appropriate.

Regulation 34
Appeals

(1) Any person aggrieved by an order or decision of the Authority under these regulations may prefer an appeal to the Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT) under section 21 of the IFSCA Act, 2019.

(2) The appeal shall be filed within 45 days of the date of receipt of the order, along with the prescribed fee.

Regulation 35
Repeal and Saving

(1) The IFSCA (Bullion Exchange) Regulations, 2020 and the IFSCA (Bullion Exchange) (Amendment) Regulations, 2021 are hereby repealed.

(2) Notwithstanding such repeal: (a) anything done or any action taken under the repealed regulations shall, to the extent consistent with these regulations, be deemed to have been done or taken under these regulations; (b) any application pending under the repealed regulations shall be treated as an application under these regulations; (c) any recognition or registration granted under the repealed regulations shall continue to be valid until it expires or is cancelled.

Annotations & Commentary

← Select any highlighted provision to read its annotation — economic logic, structural drivers, cross-references, and the most fragile assumption in each provision.

REG 1 · Short Title
Significance

BMR 2025 is a consolidation and expansion of the 2020 framework. The name change from "Bullion Exchange" Regulations to "Bullion Market" Regulations signals regulatory intent to govern the entire ecosystem — exchange, clearing, depository, vault, and access — under a single instrument.

What Changed from 2020

The 2020 regulations focused primarily on the exchange and clearing layer. BMR 2025 adds comprehensive coverage of vault managers, bullion depositories, qualified suppliers, and the DMA/sponsored access framework — reflecting IIBX's operational maturity since August 2022.

REG 2 · Definitions
Structural Weight of "Bullion Contract"

The definition of "bullion contract" (h) is deliberately broad — it encompasses BDRs, spot delivery contracts, derivatives, and products similar to BDRs. This gives IFSCA the flexibility to permit new instruments (e.g., unallocated gold accounts, lease products, forwards) without amending the principal regulation.

BDR as a Security

The BDR definition (j) is architecturally critical. By defining BDRs as a receipt created by a depository and listed on an exchange, IFSCA brings them within the SCRA 1956 framework — making them "securities" subject to securities law, not commodity law. This enables IBUs and regulated entities that cannot trade commodities to participate.

Cross-References
BMR 2020 (repealed) SCRA 1956 — s.2(h) Depositories Act 1956 IFSCA Act 2019 — s.12-13, 28
⚠ Fragile Assumption

The BDR-as-security classification depends on the SCRA 1956 reading of "security" as including receipts of depository instruments. If this is challenged — particularly for unallocated positions — the regulatory fabric unravels. The ITI notification under s.47(viiaab) has partially addressed the tax leg, but the securities law position has not been judicially tested.

REG 3 · Application
Gate Function

This provision functions as a quality gate — the information requirements in sub-regulation (2) signal the Authority's due diligence framework. The governance and infrastructure disclosures are not merely procedural; they form the factual basis on which Reg 4 conditions are assessed.

Current Position

As of 2025, IIBX is the only recognised bullion exchange and India ICC is the only recognised BCC. The application process is, in practice, a feature that could be triggered by a new entrant — but the capital and infrastructure barriers make this unlikely in the near term.

REG 4 · Recognition Conditions
Demutualisation Architecture

Sub-regulation (1)(b) mandates demutualisation — the structural separation of ownership from trading/clearing rights. This is modelled on the global post-2000 exchange reform wave (NYSE, LSE, BSE). In the bullion context, the risk being addressed is that a large bullion bank with a trading membership could, if also a significant shareholder, influence exchange policies in its own commercial interest.

Exchange vs. BCC Conditions

Sub-regulations (2) and (3) differentiate the infrastructure requirements for the exchange layer (execution, surveillance, market access) vs. the clearing layer (risk management, SGF, settlement). The bifurcation is not merely operational — it reflects a deliberate choice to keep CCP risk ring-fenced from exchange operational risk.

Cross-References
Circular 2021 — Trading Members Circular 2023 — Net worth Circular 2024 — TCM/PCM
⚠ Fragile Assumption

The CCP model assumes the SGF will be sufficient to absorb a member default. In a commodity with the price volatility of gold (historically ±3% intraday), a simultaneous default by two large clearing members could breach the SGF. There is no explicit mention of a "resolution" regime for the BCC under BMR 2025.

REG 5 · Grant of Recognition
Discretionary Conditions

Sub-regulation (2) gives IFSCA broad discretion to impose conditions at the grant stage. In practice, this is the mechanism through which many operating parameters (fee schedules, governance requirements, product approvals) are imposed via the recognition letter rather than through formal regulation. This creates a hybrid public-private regulatory instrument that is difficult to challenge.

Validity Period

The initial recognition granted to IIBX was for three years. Renewal was granted in December 2022. BMR 2025 does not specify the validity period — leaving it to IFSCA's discretion. This creates a contingent compliance obligation: all of IIBX's commercial commitments to members and clients are ultimately underwritten by a renewable regulatory licence.

REG 6 · Net Worth — Exchange
Economic Logic

The USD 5 million floor for the exchange is a skin-in-the-game requirement. It is not designed to absorb trading losses (the SGF does that) but to ensure the exchange has sufficient capital to fund technology, surveillance, governance, and business continuity. USD 5 million is modest by global exchange standards (SGX's minimum is ~USD 250 million in capital), reflecting GIFT IFSC's status as an early-stage market.

Cross-References
Circular 23 Mar 2023 — Net Worth Circular Feb 2024 — Updated
REG 7 · Net Worth — BCC
Higher Floor for BCC

The USD 10 million floor for the BCC (vs. USD 5 million for the exchange) reflects the systemic importance of the clearing function. The BCC as central counterparty novates every trade — it becomes the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer. A BCC failure is a market failure. The capital buffer here functions as a credibility signal, not as a true loss-absorption mechanism (that role belongs to the SGF and the default waterfall).

⚠ Fragile Assumption

The SGF design is not prescribed in BMR 2025 — it is left to circulars. This means the most critical risk parameter in the bullion ecosystem is sub-delegated below the principal regulation and can be changed without formal amendment.

REG 8 · Governance
Board Composition as Risk Mitigation

The governance architecture (majority PIDs + cap on shareholder and trading member directors) is designed to manage three distinct conflicts: (i) shareholder commercial interest vs. market integrity; (ii) trading member interest in lower margins vs. CCP risk management; (iii) operational management vs. regulatory accountability. The PID mechanism is the primary tool, but PIDs in IFSC entities have rarely served an adversarial compliance function — the culture is still developmental.

Circular 2021 — IIBX governance SEBI analogues — SCRA s.4
REG 9 · Public Interest Directors
IFSCA's Structural Leverage

The PID nomination power gives IFSCA a permanent presence on every exchange and BCC board. Unlike the SEBI model where PIDs are often industry nominees, IFSCA's PIDs at GIFT IFSC tend to be ex-regulators or independent technicians. In a market with 3-4 regulated entities, this creates high regulatory density — every material decision at board level is, effectively, co-made with the regulator.

REG 10 · Listing of Contracts
Product Gatekeeping

This is the most commercially consequential provision in Chapter II. The product approval mechanism (contract specifications must be approved by IFSCA) means that every new instrument — options, forwards, mini-contracts, unallocated gold accounts, silver coins — requires regulatory sign-off. This creates product development lag but also regulatory accountability for any instrument that causes losses.

Current Product Suite (as of 2025)

IIBX trades: (i) BDR-based spot contracts (Gold 1 kg, Gold 100g, Silver 30 kg); (ii) BDR mini-contracts; (iii) pilot of derivative contracts is under exploration. The DMA/sponsored access circular (July 2024) enables more sophisticated participants without product expansion.

Cross-References
Circular Jul 2024 — DMA/SA Circular Apr 2025 — Vibrance circular
REG 12 · Ownership Limits
15% Concentration Cap

The 15% single-entity cap mirrors global exchange governance norms (NYSE, HKEX). The deeper logic: a 15%+ holder can exercise disproportionate influence on board composition, thereby undermining demutualisation. The 5% cap on trading members is the tighter constraint — it prevents a dominant liquidity provider from having a structural information advantage via board access.

⚠ Fragile Assumption

In IIBX's current shareholding structure, BSE holds a significant stake. If BSE's stake approaches or exceeds the 15% threshold — or if IFSCA decides to widen the ownership regime to attract strategic investors — the cap would need to be revisited.

REG 14 · Renewal
Regulatory Leverage via Renewal

The renewal mechanism gives IFSCA substantial ongoing leverage. An exchange cannot plan multi-year capital expenditures without reasonable assurance of renewal. The three-month advance application window is short relative to infrastructure investment cycles. In practice, renewal is expected to be routine — but the theoretical possibility of non-renewal is a powerful disciplining mechanism.

REG 15 · Trading Member Eligibility
IBUs at the Centre

The inclusion of IBUs as eligible trading members is the architectural hinge of IIBX. IBUs are IFSC Banking Units — effectively foreign bank branches regulated by IFSCA/RBI operating in USD. Their participation as trading members means global bullion banks (HSBC, Standard Chartered, JP Morgan) can transact on IIBX directly, eliminating the need for domestic intermediaries.

Global Banks Circular

The August 2025 circular on "Revenue Drivers of Global Banks in Bullion Business" explicitly maps how IBUs can monetise IIBX membership through market-making, lending gold on unallocated basis, and acting as clearing members.

Cross-References
Circular Aug 2025 — Global Banks Circular Aug 2024 — TCM/PCM updated IFSCA Banking Regulations 2020
REG 16 · Registration
Exchange-Mediated Registration

Applications are routed through the exchange (not directly to IFSCA). This creates a two-stage filtration: exchange commercial judgment → IFSCA regulatory approval. The exchange has an incentive to recommend memberships (fee income) but also a reputational interest in maintaining member quality. IFSCA reserves final approval, preserving regulatory primacy.

REG 17 · Net Worth & Margins
Three-Tier Member Architecture

The TCM/PCM/SCM distinction creates a layered risk architecture. TCMs are the most common — they both trade and clear. PCMs provide clearing services to trading-only members, concentrating clearing risk in fewer, better-capitalised entities. The USD differential (SCM 1M → TCM 1.5M → PCM 3M) reflects the escalating systemic importance of each role.

Cross-References
Circular Jan 2023 — Net worth Circular Feb 2024 — Updated thresholds Circular Aug 2024 — TCM/PCM revised
REG 18 · Clearing Members
CCP Default Waterfall

The default management procedure mentioned in sub-regulation (3) is where the real systemic risk engineering happens. The standard CPMI-IOSCO framework for CCPs prescribes: initial margin → variation margin → defaulting member's default fund contribution → surviving members' default fund contributions → CCP's own capital → assessment powers. BMR 2025 delegates the design of this waterfall to circulars and BCC regulations.

⚠ Fragile Assumption

The default waterfall at IIBX/India ICC has not been publicly stress-tested. Given gold's status as a flight-to-quality asset, a clearing member default is most likely to occur during a period of extreme gold price volatility — precisely when the SGF itself is under stress.

REG 19 · Rights & Duties
AML/CFT in Bullion Context

The AML/CFT obligation for trading members (d) is particularly important for gold given its historical use in trade mis-invoicing, hawala transactions, and sanctions evasion. The FATF grey-listed India for gold-related AML deficiencies. IIBX's formal AML framework — KYC of Qualified Jewellers, transaction reporting — is part of India's response architecture.

REG 20 · Authorised Persons
Extended Access Model

The Authorised Person framework (formalised in the Sep 2025 circular) allows smaller entities — regional jewellers, smaller banks — to access IIBX through a trading member without the capital commitment of full membership. This is the IFSCA's answer to the access problem: how do you get 570+ qualified jewellers onto an exchange designed for institutional participants?

Cross-References
Circular Sep 2025 — Auth. Persons
REG 21 · DMA / Sponsored Access
Algorithmic Trading Gateway

DMA/SA (covered by the Jul 2024 circular) is the mechanism for institutional algorithmic participation. DMA allows a client to route orders directly to the exchange's matching engine without human intermediation by the broker. SA goes further — the client uses the broker's network ID. The key risk control question (who bears the risk of a rogue algorithm?) is addressed by requiring the trading member to retain full liability.

Cross-References
Circular Jul 2024 — DMA/SA framework
REG 22 · Depository Eligibility
CDSL as Bullion Depository

CDSL Commodity Repository Ltd (CCRL) is the only registered bullion depository. It is a subsidiary of CDSL, which satisfies the "promoted by a recognised depository in India" criterion. The depository function here is analogous to demat accounts in equities — except the underlying asset is physical gold in a vault rather than equity shares.

Structural Insight

The BDR as a depository receipt is the key instrument that converts physical gold into a tradeable, electronically-transferable, securities-law-governed instrument. Without the depository layer, IIBX would be a commodity exchange. With it, IIBX is a hybrid securities/commodity market with access to the full SEBI/IFSCA regulatory toolkit.

REG 23 · Depository Registration
Interface Function

The depository's four functions (BDR issuance, beneficial ownership records, vault manager interface, settlement infrastructure) make it the operational fulcrum of the IIBX ecosystem. Every physical-to-electronic conversion and every settlement in BDRs flows through the depository. Its failure would be a market-wide operational event.

REG 24 · Vault Manager Registration
Where the Metal Actually Lives

Vault managers are the physical layer of the IIBX architecture. Currently approved vault managers include BRINKS India and Sequel Logistics. The vault is located in GIFT City (Gujarat), meaning bullion imported through IIBX stays in a customs-bonded facility until taken delivery by a Qualified Jeweller — enabling it to be traded multiple times before physical delivery.

Tripartite Agreement Risk

The tripartite agreement (depositor-vault manager-depository) is the legal instrument linking physical custody to electronic records. Its enforceability in an insolvency scenario (particularly the vault manager's insolvency) is the key risk: does the depositor's claim on physical gold in a bankrupt vault survive? This is a live question in Indian insolvency law.

⚠ Fragile Assumption

The entire BDR architecture assumes that BDR-holder claims on physical gold are bankruptcy-remote from the vault manager's general creditors. This is not explicitly confirmed in Indian law and would need to be tested under the IBC 2016.

REG 25 · BDR Issuance
The Instrument Architecture

BDR creation is a three-step process: physical deposit → vault manager acknowledgement → depository electronic record. Each BDR is uniquely identified and represents a specific bar. This granularity (vs. fungible unallocated accounts) has an operational cost — it requires assay, bar-level tracking, and vault reconciliation — but it provides beneficial owner certainty that allocated accounts guarantee.

Physical Delivery Mechanism

Sub-regulation (4) — BDR surrender for physical delivery — is how Qualified Jewellers import gold. They buy BDRs on IIBX, surrender them, and take physical delivery from the vault. The DGFT/Customs mechanics of this process (duty payment, customs clearance, transport to the jeweller's premises) are prescribed in DGFT notifications and IFSCA circulars, not in BMR 2025 itself.

Cross-References
Circular Aug 2022 — BDR IT Act notification Circular Dec 2023 — QJ import process DGFT Notification 2022 — QJ scheme
REG 26 · Vault Standards
Physical Risk Layer

The vault is where systemic risk becomes physical risk. Audit reconciliation (sub-reg 2) is designed to catch discrepancies between physical stock and electronic BDR records — the classic risk in commodity warehousing (the Qingdao port aluminium fraud of 2014 is the cautionary case). The insurance requirement is the financial backstop if physical gold is lost, stolen, or destroyed.

REG 27 · Good Delivery Standards
LBMA as Global Benchmark

Adopting LBMA Good Delivery standards is the key quality-assurance mechanism. LBMA-accredited bars are accepted in all major global bullion markets — this means IIBX's BDRs represent internationally tradeable quality metal, not domestically-standard metal. This is prerequisite for attracting international market makers and Qualified Suppliers.

Qualified Supplier Privilege

LBMA-accredited suppliers (e.g., Valcambi, Heraeus, PAMP, MKS Pamp, MMTC-PAMP) deposit gold without further assay — a material operational advantage. Non-LBMA gold requires local assay and potentially re-melting/re-branding, adding cost and time.

REG 28 · Qualified Jewellers
The Political Economy of QJ Access

The "Qualified Jeweller" category is not created by BMR 2025 — it is created by the Ministry of Commerce under the DGFT framework. IFSCA merely governs how QJs access IIBX. This split jurisdiction (MoC notifies QJs; IFSCA governs their IIBX access) is the architecturally fragile point: any change in MoC policy on QJ notifications directly affects IIBX volumes, without IFSCA having control.

India's Gold Import Architecture

India imports ~800-900 tonnes of gold annually. The QJ scheme at IIBX is designed to channel a portion of this through IIBX, giving price discovery in USD in GIFT IFSC. The share of total imports through IIBX has historically been low (~5-10%) — the regulatory ambition significantly exceeds current market reality.

DGFT Notification — QJ scheme Circular Jan 2022 — First QJ circular Circular Dec 2023 — Updated QJ process RBI — FEMA directions
REG 29 · Import Process
The Cross-Border Payment Problem

Sub-regulation (3) requires FEMA compliance for payment. Gold is paid for in USD (IIBX is a USD market), but the jeweller operates in INR in India. The USD payment flows: jeweller → authorised dealer bank → IIBX settlement account → qualified supplier. Each step carries exchange rate exposure. Jewellers without USD accounts or hedging capacity face FX risk that the BDR transaction itself doesn't resolve.

Customs Complexity

Physical delivery from GIFT City vault to a jeweller's workshop in Surat or Mumbai requires: BDR surrender → customs examination → duty payment → transport. The customs duty on gold imports (currently 15% basic + 6% AIDC) significantly affects the attractiveness of IIBX vs. other import channels, and is outside IFSCA's control entirely.

REG 30 · Qualified Suppliers
Supply-Side Architecture

QSs are the international counterparties — refiners, bullion banks, and dealers that supply gold into IIBX. They are the supply-side equivalent of Qualified Jewellers. The LBMA refiner list is the effective selection mechanism: LBMA GDL has ~70 gold refiners and ~80 silver refiners globally. Empanelling them as IIBX Qualified Suppliers connects India's gold market to the global refining infrastructure without India needing to create its own standards.

Unallocated Position Privilege

Sub-regulation (4) — permitting QSs to hold unallocated positions — is the mechanism by which bullion banks can market-make on IIBX. An unallocated position is a claim against the bank for a specified quantity of gold, not a claim on specific bars. This enables efficient hedging and liquidity provision without the operational friction of allocated bar-level settlement.

Cross-References
Circular Aug 2022 — First QS list Circular Dec 2023 — Updated QS list Circular Mar 2021 — IBU unallocated
REG 31 · Obligations
AML at the Supply-Demand Interface

QJs and QSs represent both ends of the physical bullion flow: QSs bring gold in; QJs take gold out. Both are required to maintain books of accounts and comply with AML/CFT obligations. This creates a dual-sided traceability architecture — in theory, every kilogram of gold traded on IIBX can be traced from international refiner to Indian jeweller.

REG 33 · Penalties
Penalty Toolkit

IFSCA's penalty powers derive from the IFSCA Act 2019 (s.20-21). The Act provides for penalties up to INR 25 crore or three times the profits from the violation. For an IFSC entity operating in USD, a rupee-denominated penalty cap is structurally weak — its deterrent effect diminishes as IIBX volumes grow. This is a design deficit that will likely need legislative amendment.

REG 35 · Repeal and Saving
Continuity Provision

The saving clause (2) ensures that IIBX's existing recognition, India ICC's clearing recognition, and all member registrations granted under the 2020 Regulations continue without re-application. This is standard regulatory continuity architecture. The crucial question — whether prior recognition conditions survive or are superseded by BMR 2025 requirements — is answered by sub-clause (a): anything done under the 2020 Regs is deemed done under BMR 2025, to the extent consistent.

Incremental Compliance Gap

Where BMR 2025 imposes new requirements not present in the 2020 Regs (e.g., expanded governance requirements, new vault manager obligations), existing entities will need a compliance transition. BMR 2025 does not specify a transition period — this suggests IFSCA expects grandfathered entities to achieve compliance promptly.

REG 11 · Suspension & Delisting
Regulatory Override Power

Sub-regulation (2) gives IFSCA a direct override — it can compel the exchange to delist a contract even without the exchange's initiative. This is the systemic risk valve. The trigger is "systemic risk," which is undefined in BMR 2025, giving IFSCA broad discretion. In commodities context, the classic scenario is a market squeeze or a large concentrated short position that cannot be covered — IFSCA retains power to halt the contract rather than allow a disorderly market event.

Prior Approval Requirement

The exchange-initiated suspension requires prior IFSCA approval — it cannot act unilaterally. This prevents exchange management from delisting a contract for commercial reasons (e.g., low volume, fee economics) without regulatory sanction. The prior approval requirement creates regulatory accountability for every product lifecycle decision.

⚠ Fragile Assumption

There is no prescribed timeline for IFSCA to grant or refuse prior approval for suspension. In a fast-moving market event, a suspension that takes days to receive approval could cause significant damage. The absence of an emergency exception to the prior approval requirement is a structural gap.

REG 13 · Regulatory Compliance
The Surveillance Obligation

Sub-regulation (3) — the obligation to detect and prevent manipulation, wash trades, and front-running — is where the exchange takes on quasi-regulatory functions. This mirrors SEBI's model under the SCRA where the exchange is simultaneously a business entity and a front-line regulator of its members. The exchange bears liability for surveillance failures, creating strong incentives to invest in monitoring technology.

Risk Framework Architecture

The requirement for a comprehensive risk framework covering market, operational, credit, and systemic risk (sub-reg 2) is a principles-based obligation — the specifics are to be approved by IFSCA. In practice, IFSCA approval of the risk framework creates a shared accountability structure: if the framework is inadequate and a market event occurs, both the exchange and IFSCA bear reputational risk for the approved framework's failure.

REG 32 · Inspection
Supervisory Architecture

The inspection power is the operational enforcement backbone of BMR 2025. The breadth — "books of account, records and documents" — combined with access to "data and systems" means IFSCA can conduct full technology audits, not just paper-based reviews. In an exchange environment, trading system logs, order audit trails, and surveillance records are the core evidentiary material.

Delegation Pattern

IFSCA "appoints an inspecting authority" — it does not inspect directly in the first instance. This mirrors SEBI's practice of appointing external auditors or SEBI officers as inspecting authorities. The inspecting authority reports to IFSCA, which then takes action. This creates a layer of procedural insulation between fact-finding and enforcement decision-making.

Scope Includes All Regulated Entities

"Any person regulated under these regulations" captures the entire ecosystem: exchange, BCC, depository, vault managers, trading members, clearing members, QJs, and QSs. This is comprehensive supervisory reach — in theory. In practice, inspecting 570+ QJs and multiple vault managers simultaneously would require significant IFSCA resource allocation.

REG 34 · Appeals
SAT as Appellate Forum

Appeals go to the Securities Appellate Tribunal under s.21 of the IFSCA Act — the same SAT that hears SEBI appeals. This is a deliberate regulatory convergence: IIBX participants, despite operating in a USD market in an IFSC, are subject to the same appellate process as domestic securities market participants. SAT has expertise in exchange and market structure disputes — its jurisprudence on SEBI matters will inform its approach to IFSCA matters.

45-Day Limitation

The 45-day limitation for filing appeals is tight in a complex market structure dispute. Unlike civil litigation where the limitation can run to years, the short window reflects the need for finality in regulatory decisions affecting market operations. For an international participant unfamiliar with Indian procedural law, missing this window would be fatal to the appeal.

⚠ Fragile Assumption

SAT operates under Indian procedural law and its proceedings are in English but conducted in India. For a foreign bullion bank or international QS disputing an IFSCA decision, the cost and friction of SAT proceedings may make formal appeals uneconomical relative to compliance — which is a form of regulatory deterrence operating without any penalty being imposed.

Structural Observation

BMR 2025 is a consolidation-and-upgrade, not a structural overhaul. The IIBX model — exchange → CCP → depository → vault — remains architecturally unchanged from 2020. What changed is the regulatory density at each layer: more explicit conditions, more participant categories, and integration of three years' worth of operational learning from IIBX's live trading. The most significant structural addition is the vault manager and depository registration regime, which gives the physical layer for the first time a comprehensive regulatory framework in the principal regulation rather than scattered circulars.
